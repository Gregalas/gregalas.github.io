Torc

Reinventing Tech Recruitment

Project Overview

We started off 2025 with my first big project of the year - helping Torc shape the future of recruitment for tech builders by reimagining their digital talent marketplace. What makes Torc different is that they actually match talent with jobs based on their skills, as opposed to the feeding frenzy that's created by traditional job boards like LinkedIn or Indeed. So on all sides of the marketplace, things become more streamlined – Talent are served jobs they actually care about and have a good chance of landing, and recruiters in turn receive high quality talent for them to place.

What I did

Being the product design lead on this one, my main objective was to ensure the overall success of the project, at a Metalab and client level. I've been involved in all stages of the project, mainly focused in the day to day of the product UX and UI, but also overseeing and art directing the visual direction for the website alongside Matt, our exec design director.

The Challenge

Our initial scope for this project was focused around designing a vision for the talent profile that we'd eventually take into development and help Torc build over the next few months. Being a marketplace, one of the big challenges was to think about the talent, community and multiple personas on the Torc's side at all levels of the product. Bearing in mind that much of what we designed didn't exist, just the data that we could leverage, we also needed to build features and systems that create lasting engagement beyond job hunting, how to build a Torc community, and of course how to really differentiate the platform in a way that Torc could position themselves as serious contenders alongside the big dogs and avoid another clone war.

At the same time, we're also designing Torc's marketing site with a completely new visual direction which we could then translate to the new product experience. Torc was recently acquired by a massive talent company called Randstad, which has also meant that we had a slightly more nuanced stakeholder dynamic to manage.

REH and Research

After a 31 hr flight and several litres of New England's finest java, we kicked things off in Boston where I got to facilitate a 3-day workshop with the goal of consuming as much knowledge about the product and business as possible, and of course getting to know our awesome client team.

We've since run a 2 week research sprint where I worked in tandem with a UX researcher to generate concepts and learn where and how we could make the most impact for Torc over the coming months. Based on some initial hypotheses packaged in what we call an REH (Ridiculously Early Hypothesis) This basically involves me whipping quick sketch concepts that we can test and get feedback on. I love this phase of the project as it allows me to really innovate and push things without constraints while really getting to understand the problems we need to solve.

Defining the vision

Our research centered around two big questions. The first was understanding if our hypothesis of creating Career Hub that goes beyond job hunting was the right approach. We saw this as a way of building a long-term home base for Talent and setting a new standard in the industry. The other was how we should be position the profile considering that we knew we needed to move away from a static resume type approach to something more dynamic that tells a more vivid story about a candidate.

Designing for different sides of the marketplace

Profiles don't exist in isolation. They exist as part of bigger journeys to perform a task. So although we had a lot of emphasis on the profile from a scope and brief perspective, it was my job as lead to make sure profiles function within the context of the Torc system. At a scope level this means pushing beyond the initial requirements toward designing a journey as a functional prototype that we could then go and build. So a huge part of this became about understanding the unique workflows and needs of all sides of the market place and how they influence each other.

For example, when Matchers – one of the many consultants we designed for on the recruiter side – come into profiles at specific point in their workflow, they would've already narrowed down a pool of candidates in the source and match experience (essentially an applicant tracking tool). So the features we designed aimed at helping recrtuiters retain context about the job and required skills as they scan through profiles to make a decicision. We also designed for people who require different levels of depth when understanding candidate experience. For example, a recruiter scans for surface level evidence in terms of experience with a given skill, but a hiring manager looks to understand those skill at more more depth. We solved this by allowing you expose different levels of skill evidence as you ineracti with the skill.

Creating lasting engagement

Some things add huge amounts of value and are absolutely the right direction for the business to go, but those things aren't immediately feasible. Even though we had to make a few big cuts, that didn't limit our thinking on this project and it's really what made things so exciting and is helping shape Torc's future roadmap. We pushed the boundaries as much as possible, even though we knew some of the data we'd need to make it happen wasn't immediately available. In doing so we worked on growth and community features (both of which don't currently exist) for the product that elevate Torc's value proposition significantly and deliver on the promise of creating long term value for talent and recrtuiters.

For growth, I worked on a system that leverages Torc's existing awards, but in a way that helps talent feel like they're actually growing on the platform as they move to different levels. That system is tied directly to Torc engagement, community and skill related behaviour. In addition, it also introduces step-level growth, where as you validate your skills on the platform through Torc assessments, you'll increase your chances of being matched with opportunities at higher skill levels.

Avoiding clone wars (differentiation)

Aside the product thinking, one of the big differentiators was the visual direction. Torc is for people who create, innovate, and shape the digital world. So I wanted our visual approach to reflect that spirit, and develop a design language that feels functional, opinionated, and distinctly different from any other talent marketplace. The last thing we wanted to make this product feel like LinkedIn or anything that feels standard, so we had to find ways of making this product feel different but highly usable.

The visual language derives inspiration from the Torc logo. We played with the idea of using sharp edges and very dense UI to create something that feels unique, but highly functional to ensure things are scannable as possible. Something that stemmed from our research insights was this idea that recruiters want to see skills as more of a narrative, so we designed a skills timeline that tells more of a story around when and how skills were developed. It's more of a utility, that's interactive and playful, as opposed to just showing a list with some data. Through many rounds of iteration we landed on something that's super scannable and visually interesting too.

Progress

We're currently in a production phase of the project where myself and Luciano are building out the high-fidelity direction, design system and ensuring everything is responsive and works well for mobile. We're also making high-fidelity prototypes using framer and Figma to refine our interaction behaviours and experimenting with different ways of generating 3D assets for the growth section of the app.

Overall, this has been one of the most exciting projects and I've learned a ton. Being able to execute on so many different areas of design, while really getting a chance to understand the product and Torc's business deeply is exactly what I'm about and has made a huge difference in producing what we feel is a game changing product for the client.
